# The Desk: An In-House Counsel Operating Philosophy

A collection of essays on legal operations, deal closure, and building a high-performing in-house legal departments at tech companies during periods of extreme business volatility.

## Background

They say that calm seas do not make a good captain. If that is true, then the last five years have been a masterclass.

I took the helm of the Legal Department at a mid-sized Silicon Valley software company in November of 2019, anchored in the deceptive quiet of the pre-pandemic world. I was hired by one of the finest software attorneys in Silicon Valley. We shared the same "software engineer to attorney" career path – he coded in FORTRAN, and I coded in C++. He wanted to retire, and I was to be his relief. I did not know then that I was stepping onto the bridge just as the barometer was bottoming out.

What followed felt like a cascade of historical anomalies: the global paralysis of the pandemic in 2020, the supply chain fracture of 2021, the eruption of war in Europe in 2022, and the collapse of Silicon Valley Bank—our industry's financial bedrock—in 2023. By 2024, we were navigating the highest interest rates in decades.

In any normal timeline, this is a eulogy. Instead, during those exact years of tumult, we increased our deal flow and **tripled our revenue**. We did not merely survive the storm; we charted a course directly through it, capturing market share from our competitors.

This is the story of how our Legal Department facilitated this growth during this time. I expect that similar troubling times will reoccur. I feel that it is important to communicate what we did to future attorneys that find themselves in a similar situation.

## The Core Philosophy

For too long, legal departments have been viewed as the "Department of No" - a necessary evil, a tax on speed. We inverted that mode and operated on a simple, military premise: the "tooth-to-tail" ratio. In combat, you have the "tooth" (the soldier doing the fighting) and the "tail" (the logistics and support keeping them alive). In the software industry, Sales is the tooth. Everyone else—Legal, Finance, HR, Ops—is the tail.

Our job in Legal is not to police the sales team; it is to give them what they need to safely close the deal – to secure victory. When a salesperson is "engaging the enemy," like reluctant procurement officers, hostile competitors, shrinking budgets – they cannot also be fighting their own legal departments. They cannot be as effective with poor support: a contract that is too complex, or an attorney who thinks their job is to win an intellectual debate rather than close a transaction.

The strategies outlined in these pages are the ones we used to strip away friction. We weaponized autonomy, giving our staff attorneys ownership so they fought like advocates, not employees. We invested in broadcast-quality equipment, realizing that in a global/remote world, clear audio is the new firm handshake. We radically specialized, pulling every scrap of administrative work off our salespeople so they could do the one thing that matters: close deals.

The world is not getting calmer. The seas are not settling. The only answer is to build a better legal crew that understands that their desk is the engine room of the business.
